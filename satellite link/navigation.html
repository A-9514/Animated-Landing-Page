// Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS)_

GNSS constellations provide autonomous geo-spatial positioning with global coverage. Receivers on the ground calculate their position by precisely timing signals sent from multiple satellites in view.

These systems are fundamental to transportation, logistics, surveying, timing synchronization, and countless location-based services.

Operational Constellations_

GPS (Global Positioning System)

The original and most widely used GNSS, operated by the United States Space Force. Consists of ~31 operational satellites in Medium Earth Orbit (MEO), ensuring at least four are visible from almost anywhere on Earth.

Status: Fully Operational Worldwide.

GLONASS (Global Navigation Satellite System)

Russia's global navigation system. Also operates in MEO with full global coverage provided by its constellation of satellites. Many modern receivers can use both GPS and GLONASS signals for improved accuracy and reliability.

Status: Fully Operational Worldwide.

BeiDou Navigation Satellite System (BDS)

China's GNSS. Offers global services using a combination of satellites in GEO, IGSO (Inclined Geosynchronous Orbit), and MEO. Provides regional augmentation services for Asia-Pacific.

Status: Fully Operational Worldwide.

Galileo

The European Union's global navigation system, operated by the European Space Agency (ESA). Provides high-accuracy positioning and is designed for civilian use. Operates a constellation in MEO.

Status: Fully Operational Worldwide.

Regional Systems

Systems providing coverage over specific regions, often complementing global systems. Examples include India's NavIC and Japan's QZSS (which primarily augments GPS).

Status: Operational Regionally.
