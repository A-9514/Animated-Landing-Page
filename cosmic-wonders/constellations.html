Celestial Storybooks: The Constellations

For millennia, humans have gazed at the night sky, connecting stars into patterns that resemble animals, objects, or mythological figures. These patterns are called constellations.

While the stars in a constellation often appear close together, they are usually vast distances apart in reality, simply lying along the same line of sight from Earth. Today, astronomers officially recognize 88 constellations that map the entire celestial sphere.

Some Famous Star Patterns

Orion (The Hunter)

One of the most recognizable constellations, prominent in the winter sky (Northern Hemisphere). Features bright stars like Betelgeuse (a red supergiant) and Rigel (a blue supergiant), and the famous Orion Nebula, a stellar nursery.

Ursa Major (The Great Bear)

Famous for containing the Big Dipper asterism (pattern within a constellation). Visible year-round in the Northern Hemisphere. The two stars at the end of the Dipper's bowl point towards Polaris, the North Star.

Cygnus (The Swan)

Flies along the Milky Way, prominent in summer/autumn. Contains the Northern Cross asterism. Home to Deneb, one of the most luminous stars known, and Cygnus X-1, one of the first suspected black holes.

Scorpius (The Scorpion)

A large constellation prominent in the summer sky (Northern Hemisphere). Features the bright red supergiant star Antares ("Rival of Mars"). Its curved tail makes it look distinctly like a scorpion.

Canis Major (The Great Dog)

Follows Orion across the sky. Contains Sirius, the brightest star in the night sky. Known as the "Dog Star".
