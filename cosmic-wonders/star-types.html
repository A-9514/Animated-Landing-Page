A Universe of Stellar Variety

Stars come in a dazzling array of sizes, temperatures, colors, and brightness levels. Astronomers classify them primarily by their spectral type (related to temperature and color) using the sequence O, B, A, F, G, K, M ("Oh Be A Fine Guy/Girl, Kiss Me!").

Let's meet some of the main characters in the cosmic zoo:

Meet the Stars

O-Type Stars

The hottest (> 30,000 K), most massive, and brightest stars. Very blue and extremely rare. Short, dramatic lives ending in supernovae.

B-Type Stars

Still very hot (10,000–30,000 K), massive, and luminous. Blue-white in color. Live fast and die young, often ending spectacularly.

G-Type Stars (Like Our Sun)

Yellow-white stars (5,200–6,000 K). Comfortable temperature for life (as we know it!). Live for billions of years. Our own Sun is a G-type star.

K-Type Stars

Orange dwarfs (3,700–5,200 K). Cooler and dimmer than the Sun, but live much longer. Considered good candidates for hosting habitable planets.

M-Type Stars (Red Dwarfs)

The most common type! Cool (2,400–3,700 K), small, and very dim red dwarfs. Incredibly long lifespans (trillions of years).

Giants & Supergiants

Stars in later life stages that have swelled enormously (Red Giants, Blue Supergiants). They are large but not necessarily more massive than when they started.

White Dwarfs

The dense, hot remnants of low-to-average mass stars after they shed their outer layers. About the size of Earth but with the mass of the Sun!

Neutron Stars

Incredibly dense cores left after a massive star supernova. Packing more mass than the Sun into a city-sized sphere. Spin rapidly!
